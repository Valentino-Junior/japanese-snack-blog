Japan is known for its healthful cuisine that blurs the lines between gastronomy and art, but it is also known for its delicious snacks.Japanese snacks are sometimes ingenious reinterpretations of traditional meals, while other times they're influenced by European and international snack foods.

  • Japanase Mochi

    • Mochi is traditionally eaten at festivals especially at New Year's. Japanese believe that eating mochi on the first day of the year not only provides warmth and nourishment. It is also thought to impart strength, endurance and good health, to get you through the winter chill and safely through the year ahead What exactly is a mochi? The mochi cakes are beautiful circular buns made of soft and chewy rice. Rice is steamed before being pounded and mashed. The final mochi shape is made from the sticky rice mass, which is then baked or boiled.Click here to see more about packaging and where to buy them. See more

    Japanese Savoury Snacks

    Savoury snacks from Japan are famed for their distinct flavors that you won't find anywhere else! Some are even produced with Japanese ingredients such as Azumino wasabi, edamame, mentaiko (spicy cod roe), and other items that can only be found in Japan. These are always the go-to savory snack items whenever one feels "kuchisabishii," or "to eat when mouth feels empty."

  • Calbee chips

    • If you want to try salted seaweed chips, start with Calbee Norishio, as they are Japan's best-selling salted seaweed snack. Calbee potato chips come in a variety of tastes, including gently salted, shiawase "fortune" butter, consomme punch, soy sauce mayo, seaweed salt, and a variety of regional soy sauces. Even if you're inexperienced with Japanese flavors, Calbee chips are a strong, safe pick; their soy sauce mayo chips are hard to resist! Unlike Pocky, Pretz is dusted with seasonings instead of enrobed in a flavoured fudge.Pretz is a thin, seasoned biscuit stick with a pleasing crunch and irresistible appeal.   • Chicken Karaage

    • Karaage is a type of fried chicken that is only found in Japan. It's crackly crisp, juicy as only dark-meat chicken can be, with a tart zing from a squeeze of lemon, and a rich, savory aroma imparted by the duck fat. Karaage is a popular Japanese snack that may be found in convenience stores around the country, as well as in izakaya and more substantial meals like rice bowls and curries.Karaage are always in a small ball shape, making them convenient to eat in a single bite and perfect for putting on a skewer.The lightness of karaage sets it apart from other types of fried chicken. Karaage is powdered, soft, and fluffy; it isn't heavy, oily, or dense in the way it feels or tastes.
